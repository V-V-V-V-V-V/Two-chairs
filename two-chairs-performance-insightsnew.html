01 · The period in brief

The campaign is generating strong engagement. Washington is converting it; California is the next unlock.

Across 23 days and three paid channels, the program delivered 516.7K impressions, 7,068 clicks, and 14 apply finishes. Washington produced all 14 apply finishes — 9 on LinkedIn (6 in May, 3 in June) and 5 on Meta (June). California drew strong engagement (53% of total clicks, higher Meta CTR than WA) but those clicks haven't converted yet. Our analysis below shows the message that resonates in each market and the platform-level changes we're rolling out to lift California into the conversion column.

"Washington is converting; California is engaging. The next cycle is about closing the gap — and we have clear, channel-specific moves to do it."

Three Meta improvements rolling out:

1. Separate the campaign by placement. We'll split the single campaign into placement-specific campaigns (Audience Network · Reels · Feed · Stories) so budget and bidding can be controlled independently per placement. Today all placements compete in the same auction logic, which lets the cheapest (Audience Network) absorb most of the budget by default.

2. Restrict low-CTR placements. Once campaigns are split, we'll cap or remove Audience Network and redirect that budget to higher-CTR placements (Instagram Reels at 1.70%, Instagram Stories at 2.45%, Instagram Feed at 1.88%).

3. A/B test traffic optimisation vs conversion optimisation. Today the campaign optimises for traffic (clicks). With Meta now producing apply finishes (5 in June), we have enough conversion signal to test conversion-optimised bidding head-to-head against the current traffic-optimised setup. We'll split spend across the two and read which produces a better cost-per-apply.

4. Tighten age targeting to 18–54. 65+ absorbs 27% of impressions at the worst CTR — excluding 65+ recovers budget for the engaged segments, especially important once Audience Network is restricted.

How we close the California gap:

1. Match the value prop to the market. Lead CA creative with Compensation & Autonomy on LinkedIn and Compensation on Meta — the themes that posted the highest CA CTRs. Keep the WA-resonant themes (Clinical Community & Training, Reduced Burnout) running in WA.

2. Improve Meta placement quality. Separate the Meta campaign by placement and restrict Audience Network — CA is over-indexed on AN impressions, which is the lowest-CTR placement. Moving CA's Meta delivery into Instagram Reels / Feed / Stories should lift click quality.

3. Layer LinkedIn Sponsored InMail. Direct-to-inbox delivery to CA-targeted therapist titles, carrying the Compensation & Autonomy message — historically a stronger conversion format than feed-based Sponsored Content.

4. Bring CA into Google's new format launches. AI Max with text customisation and Performance Max will both run in CA and WA — broadening the surface area for high-intent CA candidates.
06 · What we'll improve — next-cycle plan

Seven specific moves we're rolling out across the three channels.

Organised by channel. Most of these redirect existing budget into higher-performing surfaces, formats, and creative themes; point 7 specifically reallocates spend across themes — scaling the winners on each channel and cutting the underperformers.

1Separate the Meta campaign by placementMETA
We'll split the single Meta campaign into placement-specific campaigns — Audience Network, Instagram Reels, Instagram Feed, Instagram Stories, Facebook Feed — so each placement has its own budget and bidding logic. Today all placements compete in the same auction, which lets the cheapest (Audience Network) absorb most impression share by default. Separating gives us per-placement control.
2Restrict Audience Network and reallocate to higher-CTR placementsMETA
Audience Network posts a lower CTR than every in-feed placement (1.12% on AN vs 1.70%+ on Instagram Reels, Feed, and Stories). Once campaigns are split, we'll cap or remove AN and redirect that budget into Instagram Reels / Feed / Stories where engagement is materially stronger.
3A/B test traffic optimisation vs conversion optimisationMETA
Today the Meta campaign optimises for traffic (link clicks). With Meta now producing apply finishes (5 in June), we have enough conversion signal to test conversion-optimised bidding head-to-head against traffic-optimised bidding. We'll split spend between the two setups on identical creative and audience, and read which produces a better cost-per-apply finish.
4Launch AI Max with exact-match + text customisationGOOGLE
We'll move our exact-match keyword set onto Google's AI Max framework with text customisation turned on. This lets Google's AI tailor the ad copy dynamically based on (a) the user's specific search query, (b) their intent signals, and (c) the landing page content — so someone searching "LCSW jobs Sacramento" sees ad copy that emphasises LCSW + the Sacramento page, while "therapy careers remote" sees a different tailored version. Same keyword precision, AI-optimised creative on top.
5Launch Performance Max on GoogleGOOGLE
We'll launch a Performance Max campaign to extend our reach into YouTube, Discover, Gmail, Maps, and Search Partners — Google-owned surfaces our keyword-targeted Search campaign doesn't cover. PMax uses Google's AI to find candidates across all surfaces using our existing creative assets. This is the multi-surface complement to our keyword-targeted Search activity, giving us a second high-intent demand stream from Google.
6Add LinkedIn Sponsored InMailLINKEDIN
LinkedIn Sponsored Content is already our conversion engine (9 of 14 applies). We're layering Sponsored InMail on top because in prior recruitment campaigns we've seen materially better conversion rates with InMail vs Sponsored Content alone — direct-to-inbox delivery to targeted therapist titles, read in a 1:1 context rather than a feed scroll. We'll run InMail with the CA-resonant themes (Compensation & Autonomy) and the WA-resonant themes (Clinical Community & Training) and measure apply-finish rate against the Sponsored Content baseline.
7Reallocate budget toward the winning creative themes — and pull spend off the underperformersCREATIVE BUDGET
The theme-level data shows clear winners and clear underperformers on each channel. We'll redirect budget accordingly:

Scale up where it's working:
· Reduced Burnout on Meta — the most efficient theme (1.30% CTR · $0.23 CPC), currently only ~25% of Meta spend despite outperforming Compensation on both CTR and CPC.
· Clinical Community & Training on LinkedIn (WA) — the highest-CTR cell in the entire program (3.12%). Increase WA LinkedIn budget weighted to this theme.
· Compensation & Autonomy on LinkedIn (CA) — the highest-CTR cell for California (2.49%). Increase CA LinkedIn budget weighted to this theme.

Cut spend where it's not working:
· Clinical Community & Training on Meta — 0.81% CTR, the weakest theme on the channel. Pull off Meta entirely; the theme works on LinkedIn, not in a feed-scroll context.
· Reduced Burnout on LinkedIn — 1.37% CTR, the weakest theme on the channel. Pull off LinkedIn; the theme works on Meta, not in a professional-context.

Net effect: same total creative budget, redistributed so each theme runs only on the channel where it actually performs.
Bottom line: The campaign foundation is solid — reach, engagement, and message-fit signals are all reading clearly. The next cycle's work is execution: better Meta placements, Google's two new formats, LinkedIn InMail as the new conversion layer, and creative budget weighted toward the themes that are working on each channel. We expect California to move into the apply-finish column on the back of these changes.
